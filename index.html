Abstract

Scientific breakthroughs often emerge by synthesizing prior ideas into novel contributions. GIANTS asks whether language models can emulate this targeted form of scientific progress. We introduce insight anticipation, a literature-grounded generation task in which a model receives summaries of two parent papers and must predict the core contribution of a downstream paper that builds on them. To study this setting, we construct GiantsBench, a benchmark of 17,839 parent-downstream tuples spanning eight broad scientific areas, and evaluate generations with an LM judge whose scores correlate with human expert ratings. We also present GIANTS-4B, a 4B-parameter model trained with reinforcement learning to optimize semantic similarity to the target insight. Despite its smaller open architecture, GIANTS-4B outperforms both supervised baselines and larger proprietary models, including a 34% relative gain over Gemini-3-Pro on the main test set.

Insight Anticipation

GIANTS reframes automated scientific discovery as targeted synthesis rather than unconstrained brainstorming. The core question is simple: once the relevant lineage is fixed, can a model infer the next scientific leap?

Formally, each example maps two parent-paper summaries (x_A, x_B) to a standalone target insight y*. This setup isolates insight generation from the harder retrieval problem of deciding which prior work matters, making it possible to measure whether a model can combine two foundations into a coherent, non-obvious downstream contribution.

Input Context

Two parent-paper summaries whose ideas are jointly required for the downstream result.

Oracle Target

A rewritten, self-contained statement of the downstream paper's core contribution.

Evaluation

A 1-10 LM-judge similarity score comparing the generated insight to the ground-truth target.

GiantsBench

GiantsBench is built from 17,839 arXiv papers whose latest update dates fall between May 23, 2007 and January 23, 2026. The pipeline keeps papers with at least two citations, uses Gemini-2.5-Flash to identify two explicitly cited parent papers and explain their synergy, summarizes each parent paper, and rewrites the resulting downstream contribution into a standalone target insight.

The main split is temporal. Training uses only cs.CL papers published before July 1, 2023, yielding 10,335 examples. Evaluation uses 7,504 later papers across domains, plus a stricter 5,294-example Test-unseen-parents split where evaluation parent papers never appear during training.

Training GIANTS-4B

GIANTS-4B starts from Qwen3-4B and compares three learning strategies: direct supervised fine-tuning on the target insight, supervised fine-tuning with synthetic reasoning traces, and GRPO-based reinforcement learning that optimizes semantic similarity to the downstream contribution.

The central training result is that imitation alone is not enough. Plain SFT and SFT-think yield only modest gains, whereas RL substantially improves performance by sampling candidate insights, scoring them with an LM judge, and reinforcing the most semantically faithful generations. To limit reward overfitting, training uses Gemini-2.5-Flash as the reward model, while final evaluation is reported with separate judges including Gemini-3-Pro and Qwen3-14B.

Experimental Results

Insight anticipation is difficult even for strong frontier models: Gemini-2.5-Pro, Gemini-3-Pro, and the Qwen3-4B base model all score in the mid-4s on a 10-point similarity scale. Reinforcement learning changes that picture, lifting GIANTS-4B to 5.97 on the full test set and 6.11 on Test-unseen-parents.

Qualitative Comparison

The qualitative examples show the same pattern as the aggregate results. The base model often either paraphrases the parent papers or makes claims that outrun the evidence, whereas GIANTS-4B proposes narrower, mechanistic hypotheses that stay grounded in the cited lineage.

Example generations. On two NeurIPS 2025 award-winning lineages, GIANTS-4B recovers concrete cross-paper mechanisms and avoids the overly ambitious, less feasible extrapolations produced by the base model.

Prompt Pipeline

The dataset and training loop are driven by four coordinated prompts: parent identification, target insight rewriting, insight generation, and similarity judging.

Parent identification prompt. Gemini-2.5-Flash identifies two cited parent papers whose ideas are synergistically combined by the downstream work.

Rewrite prompt. The raw synergy explanation is rewritten into a clean, standalone target insight that does not explicitly mention the downstream paper.

Generation prompt. This is the core task prompt used for training and evaluation: generate a specific, non-obvious, self-contained insight from two parent summaries.

Judge prompt. The judge assigns a 1-10 similarity score to a generated insight relative to the ground-truth target; the same rubric is used as a reward signal during RL with a separate training judge.

Limitations and Future Work

This formulation is intentionally conservative. Each downstream paper is reduced to two parent papers for tractability, even though real scientific advances often draw on broader and noisier intellectual lineages. Citations are only an imperfect proxy for conceptual ancestry, and the benchmark assumes the relevant parents have already been identified.

The natural next step is an end-to-end discovery system that retrieves promising parents, proposes candidate insights, and evaluates them for novelty, plausibility, and scientific value within a single loop.
